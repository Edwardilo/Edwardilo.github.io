CFD & Fluid Mechanics

Case Study

Impinging Jet Flow: Analytical and Numerical Investigation

Ontario Tech University  ·  Fluid Mechanics  ·  2025

SolidWorks Flow Simulation CFD Fluid Mechanics Analytical Modelling Hiemenz Flow Turbulence

Part 1

The Problem

Impinging jet flows appear across a wide range of engineering applications: cooling electronic components, drying industrial surfaces, and directing thrust in propulsion systems. In every case, the same physical sequence plays out. A high-speed jet issues from a nozzle, travels through a free-jet region, strikes a surface, and then turns and spreads radially outward as a wall jet. The question is how accurately that behaviour can be predicted, and where the limits of a simplified model lie.

This project investigated a turbulent circular air jet issuing from a 20 mm pipe and impinging normally on a flat plate at a nozzle-to-plate distance of 60 mm, giving a Reynolds number of approximately 13,200. The objective was to analyse the flow using both classical analytical theory and a numerical CFD simulation, then directly compare the two to understand where each approach is reliable and where it is not.

CFD Velocity Trajectories Overview

Part 2

The Approach

The analytical side of the study applied classical impinging jet theory. The stagnation region was modelled using the Hiemenz velocity field, which treats the flow near the impingement point as a local straining flow with a strain rate derived directly from the inlet velocity and nozzle spacing. From there, the pressure distribution along the plate, the radial wall-jet velocity decay, the boundary layer thickness at several radial positions, and the total momentum force on the plate were all computed from first principles.

The numerical model was built in SolidWorks Flow Simulation using the same geometry: a vertical pipe above an enclosed flat plate with surrounding side walls. A global mesh with local refinement near the jet and impingement zone was used, producing a 43,688-cell model. Global goals for average pressure and average velocity were monitored throughout the run. The solver converged after 184 iterations with no warnings, with the monitored average velocity stabilising at approximately 3.14 m/s.

Centre-Plane Pressure Distribution

Part 3

The Results and What They Revealed

The CFD results confirmed the qualitative structure predicted by the analytical model: a high-pressure stagnation zone directly beneath the pipe, radial outflow spreading along the plate, and velocity decay with increasing radial distance. Quantitative agreement was reasonable for stagnation pressure, with the CFD reporting a local plate surface pressure of approximately 104,091 Pa against the analytical prediction of 101,385 Pa.

The more instructive findings came from what the analytical model could not capture. The CFD geometry included side walls and a finite enclosure, and the results showed clear confinement-induced recirculation: once the wall jet reached the boundaries, flow was redirected upward and secondary circulation cells developed inside the enclosure. None of this appears in the analytical solution, which assumes ideal radial spreading over an unbounded plate. The comparison made it evident that analytical models are powerful tools for understanding the physics and establishing baseline estimates, but CFD becomes necessary as soon as geometry, confinement, or three-dimensional effects become significant.

Robotics & Control

Case Study

PID Controller Optimisation via Genetic Algorithm

Ontario Tech University  ·  Graduate Research  ·  2025

MATLAB Simulink PID Control Genetic Algorithm 2-DOF Robot Arm Dynamic Modelling

Part 1

The Problem

A PID controller works by continuously calculating the error between a desired setpoint and a measured output, then applying a corrective input based on three terms: proportional, integral, and derivative. The challenge is in selecting the right values for each of those three gains. Set them poorly and the system either responds too slowly, oscillates, or overshoots its target before settling. For a two-joint robot arm where both joints interact dynamically, the problem compounds: the motion of one joint creates torques that directly affect the other, making manual tuning a slow and imprecise process.

The goal of this project was to replace that manual process with a structured optimisation method. Rather than adjusting gains by hand and observing the result, we used a Genetic Algorithm to search the parameter space systematically and find the gain combinations that produced the best overall system response.

Simulink Block Diagram

Part 2

The Approach

The system was modelled in MATLAB/Simulink as a two-degree-of-freedom robot arm, with each joint controlled by its own PID controller. The Simulink model captured the full nonlinear dynamics of the arm: the DynamicModel subsystem computed the torques required at each joint based on the current joint angles, velocities, and the applied control inputs, while the RobotArm subsystem propagated those torques forward through the equations of motion to produce the resulting joint accelerations and positions.

The Genetic Algorithm operated on this model by treating each set of PID gains as a candidate solution. At each generation, the GA evaluated a population of gain sets by simulating the system and measuring how well each set minimised a fitness function based on the integrated error across both joints. The best-performing candidates were selected, recombined, and mutated to produce the next generation. Over successive generations, the population converged toward gain values that consistently produced fast, stable, low-overshoot responses for both joints simultaneously.

Part 3

The Outcome

The GA-tuned controllers outperformed manually tuned equivalents across the key performance metrics: reduced overshoot, shorter settling time, and lower steady-state error for both joints. The optimisation also highlighted something that manual tuning often misses: the interaction between the two joints meant that gains optimal for one joint in isolation were not optimal when both were running together. The GA naturally accounted for this coupling because it evaluated both joints simultaneously within the same fitness function.

From an engineering standpoint, the more significant result was demonstrating that a metaheuristic search method could reliably handle a multi-input, coupled dynamic system where the solution space is non-convex and traditional analytical tuning methods are impractical. The full model, code, and simulation results are available in the project repository.

Case Study

Battery Charging Station: Kinetics Africa

Kinetics Africa / Afripoint Integrated  ·  Mechanical Design Engineer  ·  2023

SolidWorks Sheet Metal Assembly Design Product Development BOM Prototype

Part 1

The Brief

Kinetics Africa, a Nigerian clean energy startup under Afripoint Integrated, was developing an electric tricycle for commercial release. They had a working prototype of the vehicle and the battery units that would power it. What they did not yet have was a way to charge those batteries at scale. The brief was clear: design a charging station that could house and charge multiple tricycle batteries simultaneously, with each bay secured and metered independently.

The starting point was not a specification sheet or a catalogue component. It was the battery itself. I took direct measurements of the physical prototype battery, and every dimension in the design flowed from those numbers. This is what prototype work looks like in a startup environment: there are no established standards yet, no existing infrastructure to reference, just a physical object on a workbench and a problem to solve around it.

Assembly Drawing with Bill of Materials

Part 2

The Design

The station was designed in SolidWorks as a full assembly: a steel enclosure subdivided into 15 individual bays by a grid of horizontal and vertical partitions. Each bay was sized precisely to accept the battery, with clearance for the charging cable and enough depth to allow the door to close flush. Every compartment was fitted with its own charger, power meter, keypad, and a dual-lock door to ensure that only the authorised user could access their battery.

Designing around a prototype battery introduced a constraint that standard product design rarely faces: nothing was fixed. The battery dimensions were from a prototype, which meant tolerances had to account for variation in future production units. The compartment sizing and partition spacing were deliberately kept slightly generous to ensure the design would remain valid even if the final battery dimensions shifted modestly from the prototype values.

3D Assembly Render

Part 3

The Deliverable

The project output was a complete SolidWorks assembly with fully detailed engineering drawings and a Bill of Materials covering all 13 unique component types across the station. For a startup at prototype stage, these drawings serve a dual purpose: they are the fabrication documents for the physical prototype, and they are the baseline design record from which the product will be refined as development continues.

Working in a startup means the design does not exist in isolation. It has to be communicated clearly to fabricators who are being briefed for the first time, costed against a budget that is still being established, and understood by stakeholders who are evaluating whether to move the product forward. A drawing set that is unambiguous and complete is not just a technical document in that context — it is part of the case for the product itself.

CAD & Enclosure Design

Case Study

Electrical Panel Enclosure: Compass Series

Mikano International Limited  ·  Mechanical Design Engineer  ·  2022

SolidWorks Sheet Metal DXF GD&T Assembly Drawings BOM

Part 1

The Problem and the Thinking Behind It

The client required an electrical panel installed outdoors, wall-mounted on an exposed exterior surface. That requirement immediately changed the nature of the design brief. The electrical components inside (busbars, circuit breakers, terminal blocks) could not be directly exposed to outdoor conditions. The solution was to house the inner electrical panel within a larger outer enclosure that would provide the weatherproofing and structural protection needed for an outdoor installation.

The more involved challenge was figuring out how to mount the inner panel securely inside the outer enclosure without compromising access for future maintenance. Simply bolting it flat against the back plate was not feasible given the depth constraints and the need for cable entry from below. After reviewing the available options, I settled on a mounting clamp arrangement: a set of fabricated clamps that fix the inner panel to the mounting rails of the outer enclosure, holding it firmly in position while still allowing it to be released and removed if servicing requires it.

Assembly Overview

Part 2

The Design and Its Constraints

Designing in sheet metal demands a different approach from standard solid modelling. Bend radii, material springback, punch clearances, and minimum flange lengths all impose constraints that have to be respected from the first sketch, not resolved at the end. The enclosures were designed in 1.5 mm mild steel using SolidWorks Sheet Metal tools, which allowed all flat patterns and bend allowances to be derived directly from the 3D model.

One of the more deliberate decisions in the design was the clearance provided between the mounting panel and the wall surface. When a panel is bolted to a wall, the fixing bolts need to remain accessible for torquing during installation, re-tightening after settlement, or full removal during relocation. Without adequate clearance behind the mounting flanges, a standard socket or wrench simply cannot reach the fasteners. The design accounted for this explicitly, ensuring sufficient standoff depth at each fixing point to allow unobstructed tool access without requiring the inner panel to be removed first.

Mounting Detail

Part 3

The Drawing Package

A three-dimensional model communicates geometry well, but it does not constitute a manufacturing document. The drawing package is the formal record of engineering intent: the document that the fabrication shop works from, the client approves, and the site technician references during installation. For the Compass series, a complete set of 2D drawings was produced, including general assembly drawings with overall envelope dimensions, individual part drawings with tolerances and surface finish callouts, and DXF flat-pattern files formatted for CNC punching and laser cutting.

Dimensional accuracy in the drawings carries direct consequences in the field. A missing tolerance, an ambiguous datum, or an incorrectly projected view can result in parts that do not fit, fastener holes that do not align, or clearances that are insufficient once assembled. Each drawing was cross-referenced against the model and reviewed for completeness before issue. The objective was to produce a drawing set clear enough that a fabricator with no prior knowledge of the project could manufacture every component to specification, and a technician could install the finished assembly without requiring further clarification.

Even a component as seemingly straightforward as an enclosure panel demands precision at every level. A single misapplied tolerance or an overlooked geometric constraint can mean a part that cannot be assembled, a door that does not close flush, or a mounting hole that misses its mating feature by a fraction of a millimetre. GD&T provides the language to communicate those requirements unambiguously, ensuring that what was designed is exactly what gets built.
