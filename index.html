CAD & Enclosure Design

Case Study

Electrical Panel Enclosure — Compass Series

Mikano International Limited  ·  Mechanical Design Engineer  ·  2022

SolidWorks Sheet Metal DXF GD&T Assembly Drawings BOM

Part 1

The Problem and the Thinking Behind It

The client required an electrical panel installed outdoors, wall-mounted on an exposed exterior surface. That requirement immediately changed the nature of the design brief. The electrical components inside — busbars, circuit breakers, terminal blocks — could not be directly exposed to outdoor conditions. The solution was to house the inner electrical panel within a larger outer enclosure that would provide the weatherproofing and structural protection needed for an outdoor installation.

The more involved challenge was figuring out how to mount the inner panel securely inside the outer enclosure without compromising access for future maintenance. Simply bolting it flat against the back plate was not feasible given the depth constraints and the need for cable entry from below. After reviewing the available options, I settled on a mounting clamp arrangement — a set of fabricated clamps that fix the inner panel to the mounting rails of the outer enclosure, holding it firmly in position while still allowing it to be released and removed if servicing requires it.

Assembly Overview

Part 2

The Design and Its Constraints

Designing in sheet metal demands a different approach from standard solid modelling. Bend radii, material springback, punch clearances, and minimum flange lengths all impose constraints that have to be respected from the first sketch — not resolved at the end. The enclosures were designed in 1.5 mm mild steel using SolidWorks Sheet Metal tools, which allowed all flat patterns and bend allowances to be derived directly from the 3D model.

One of the more deliberate decisions in the design was the clearance provided between the mounting panel and the wall surface. When a panel is bolted to a wall, those fixing bolts eventually need to be accessed — whether to torque them during installation, re-tighten them after settlement, or remove the panel entirely for relocation or replacement. Without adequate clearance behind the mounting flanges, a standard socket or wrench simply cannot reach the fasteners. The design accounted for this explicitly, ensuring sufficient standoff depth at each fixing point to allow unobstructed tool access without requiring the inner panel to be removed first.

Mounting Detail

Part 3

The Drawing Package

A three-dimensional model communicates geometry well, but it does not constitute a manufacturing document. The drawing package is the formal record of engineering intent — the document that the fabrication shop works from, the client approves, and the site technician references during installation. For the Compass series, a complete set of 2D drawings was produced: general assembly drawings with overall envelope dimensions, individual part drawings with tolerances and surface finish callouts, and DXF flat-pattern files formatted for CNC punching and laser cutting.

Dimensional accuracy in the drawings carries direct consequences in the field. A missing tolerance, an ambiguous datum, or an incorrectly projected view can lead to parts that do not fit, fastener holes that do not align, or clearances that are insufficient once assembled. Each drawing was cross-referenced against the model and reviewed for completeness before issue. The objective was to produce a drawing set clear enough that a fabricator with no prior knowledge of the project could manufacture every component to specification, and a technician could install the finished assembly without requiring further clarification.
